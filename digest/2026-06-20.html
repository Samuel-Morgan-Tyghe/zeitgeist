"Agentic" peaks. Evals + verifiability are the real disciplines.

In plain English

For a year the AI conversation has been about making computers more independent — programs that "do things for you" on their own. The most respected people in the field are now quietly saying that's not the real problem any more. The hard part is knowing whether the computer actually did the job — and remembering what it did between Tuesday and Wednesday.

02 ·Today's reading six pieces · plain English first, then the named version

  1. 01
    Evals are the new skill Hamel Husain & Shreya Shankar · Lenny's Newsletter

    For decades writing software meant if input X, then output Y — clean checks. AI now does work where you can't write that check upfront, so the new craft is designing the test: rubrics for what counts as a good answer, a judge model to score outputs, a regression suite that catches drift between model versions. The engineers who can do this well are suddenly the most valuable people in the room.

    Hamel + Shreya position evals as "the product-design discipline" — the gate that decides what's actually shippable. Karpathy's "automate what you can verify" is the same idea on the other side of the equation: phases of the SDLC with strong eval signals (unit tests, builds, types) automate first; phases without (architecture, taste) stay human. If you only build one capability in 2026, build this one.

  2. 02
    40% of MCP servers, no auth VIPER-MCP · arXiv 2605.21392

    A lot of new AI tools plug into chatbots using a protocol called MCP (think USB for AI tools). Researchers scanned 40,000 public MCP servers and found roughly four in ten ship with no authentication at all — and they uncovered 106 previously-unknown security holes. The short version: a malicious page can quietly steer what your chatbot does and reads on your behalf.

    The NSA has now flagged the underlying pattern as the "lethal trifecta": untrusted input + tool access + outbound channel = data-exfiltration vector. EchoLeak on Microsoft 365 is the canonical in-the-wild example. If you operate or depend on any MCP server in production, this is Monday's work — audit auth + scope every tool to least privilege.

  3. 03
    OpenAI buys the runtime OpenAI · acquisition of Ona

    For a year people assumed the AI race was about whoever builds the smartest model. OpenAI just paid for a company that does something more boring and more important: it lets AIs live persistently between sessions, with audit logs of what they did. The bet is that the real prize isn't a smarter brain — it's giving the brain a place to stay and a record of what it did Tuesday.

    The acquisition of Ona signals the next infrastructure layer: long-running agent runtime, persistence, audit trails, memory governance. JPMorgan framed it as "the era of long-running agents." The market's view of where enterprise unlock sits has moved from foundation-model parameters to runtime + memory + provenance.

  4. 04
    Skills > docs Drew Breunig · dbreunig.com

    Nobody likes writing documentation, but every engineer is now writing "skills" — small step-by-step files that tell an AI how to do a specific job. It's the same labour as docs. Except this time the AI actually reads it and follows the steps, so people will finally do the work.

    Breunig's line: "no one wanted to write docs, but everyone is writing skills." Skills are executable docs scoped to an agent's tool-use surface. Quiet leading indicator — the shape of organisational knowledge is moving from human-readable prose to agent-executable instruction. Watch for the corollary: who owns the skill registry inside a company, and how is it versioned?

  5. 05
    The keeper frame of 2026 Andrej Karpathy · Sequoia AI Ascent 2026

    Karpathy gave the cleanest one-sentence test for whether AI can do a job: "automate what you can verify." If you have a clean way to check the result — tests, builds, lints, types — the AI can take the job. If you don't — architecture taste, what to build, what to ship — keep a human on it. Every will AI replace X? debate collapses to this single question.

    Full Karpathy: "Traditional computers automate what you can specify in code. This latest round of LLMs can automate what you can verify." The SDLC phases with strong verification signals automate first; the rest stay human because eval signals are absent. Patrick Roos's clean restatement: Model + Harness = Agent — the harness is the engineering work of expanding what's verifiable, which is where leverage compounds.

  6. 06
    Ontology, tempered Palantir · Juan Sequeda

    A small group of forward thinkers say the real fix for AI's tendency to make things up isn't a smarter model — it's giving the AI a structured map of the world (an "ontology") so it knows what entities exist, how they relate, and can't invent nonsense. The idea is real. The catch: Palantir has been saying it since 2023 and the rest of the field is just now catching up. Slow burn.

    Ontology-grounded agents / KG-grounded retrieval as the structured-world-model counter to brittle agentic/loops frameworks. Palantir, Neo4j, and Sequeda have the real production traction; the rest is positioning. Tempered because the time-to-adoption curve here is years, not quarters — be early on the concept, don't declare victory or build a wedge on the timeline.

03 ·Skip the hype

"Agentic [anything]" — the word now signals nothing about autonomy or reliability. Ask "what's the verification signal?" not "is it agentic?"
"AI fully replaces developers" — a fresh study (arXiv:2606.07448) shows AI-commit adoption more than doubled in new projects, yet only 0–20% of dev work is fully delegatable — the judgment calls stay human.
